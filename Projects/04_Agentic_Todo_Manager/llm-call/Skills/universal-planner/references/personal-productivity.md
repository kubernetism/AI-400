# Personal Productivity Planning Reference

Use when the plan is for an individual's personal or professional productivity: daily/weekly planning, habit building, career development, or time management.

---

## Time Management Frameworks

### The MIT Method (Most Important Tasks)
- Choose 1–3 Most Important Tasks per day
- Do MIT first, before checking email/messages
- Everything else is secondary

### Time Blocking
- Divide the day into blocks of focused work
- Assign specific tasks to specific time blocks
- Include buffer blocks (15–30 min) between major blocks
- Reserve "deep work" blocks for complex tasks

### Energy Management
Map tasks to your energy curve:
| Energy Level | Best Tasks |
|---|---|
| High (peak hours) | Deep work, complex problems, creative work |
| Medium | Meetings, emails, reviews, planning |
| Low | Admin, filing, casual browsing, breaks |

For most people: Peak = morning (7–11 AM), Trough = early afternoon (1–3 PM), Recovery = late afternoon (4–6 PM)

## Weekly Planning Template (Sunday/Friday Review)

```
WEEKLY REVIEW (15 min)
1. What did I complete this week? ✅
2. What didn't get done? Why?
3. What are my Top 3 goals for next week?

WEEKLY PREVIEW (15 min)
1. Block time for Top 3 goals first
2. Schedule meetings, deadlines
3. Identify potential blockers
4. What can I delegate or drop?
```

## Habit Stacking Formula

New Habit = After [EXISTING HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT] for [DURATION].

Example: "After Fajr prayer, I will review my MIT list for 10 minutes."

## Pakistan-Specific Productivity Considerations

- **Prayer Times**: Block 5 × 15-min prayer breaks. Don't schedule back-to-back meetings across prayer times.
- **Load Shedding**: Keep offline backup for critical work; schedule online tasks during peak power hours
- **Jumu'ah**: Block 12:00–2:00 PM every Friday for prayer + travel
- **Ramadan**: Shift schedule — deep work after Sehri (pre-dawn) or post-Taraweeh
- **Eid**: Plan 3–5 day productivity gap around Eid ul-Fitr and Eid ul-Adha
